In March 2010, a new application, Burbn, was launched as a rival to the popular Foursquare app. It offered a few added features to stand out amidst other location-based applications being offered at the time.

Despite exhibiting moderate user growth, it soon became apparent that the app's complexity, fueled by its additional features, was overwhelming for users.

The founders decided to analyze their userâ€™s engagement with the application to better understand how their product was being used.

Upon examining the engagement metrics, the founders observed a surprising trend: the primary feature, location check-in, was largely ignored. Instead, users were predominantly using the photo-sharing feature, originally added as one of many unique features to the app. The founders knew something needed to change if they were going to build a successful product for their users.

Recognizing this opportunity, the founders conducted an extensive analysis of prevailing photography apps, notably Hipstamatic and Facebook. While Hipstamatic boasted many users and great functionality, it lacked easy photo-sharing.

Conversely, Facebook's photo-sharing feature on its iPhone app was lagging in performance during that period. Guided by these insights and their users' trends, the founders decided to pivot, concentrating solely on photo-sharing.

Believing they could carve a niche in the market with a user-friendly photo-sharing app, they developed a robust photo-sharing infrastructure and eliminated almost every other other feature from the application. Rebranded as Instagram, the streamlined app was launched in October that year.

The new application was a huge success. Within two months, Instagram amassed an impressive one million registered users and continued to grow exponentially. Barely two years into its remarkable journey, Instagram was acquired by Facebook for a whopping one billion dollars.
